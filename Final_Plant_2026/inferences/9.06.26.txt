After observing that the controller response remained almost identical regardless of whether the TD3 agent was connected or disconnected, we began a systematic debugging process to determine why the reinforcement learning agent was having virtually no influence on the plant. First, we verified that the observations entering the RL Agent were correct by confirming that both the control error and Disturbance A were continuously changing during simulation. Next, we logged the TD3 outputs α and β and discovered that they remained fixed at exactly 0.5 throughout the entire simulation, despite the observations changing significantly. This immediately indicated that the actor network was not producing meaningful adaptive actions. To investigate further, we manually queried the trained actor using `getAction()` with several completely different states such as `[0;15]`, `[100;15]`, and `[-100;15]`, and found that the actor always returned `[0.5;0.5]`, proving that the policy had collapsed to a constant action. We then inspected the actor architecture and discovered the root cause: the network ended with a `tanhLayer`, which naturally outputs values in the range `[-1,1]`, while the action space was defined as `[0.5,2.0]`. Since there was no scaling layer to map the tanh output into the valid action range, MATLAB continuously clipped many actor outputs to the lower bound of 0.5, causing α and β to become permanently stuck at their minimum values. As a result, the adaptive PI controller never truly adapted, reward function changes had no visible effect, training appeared ineffective, and the controller response remained almost identical even after 100 episodes of training. To solve this, we redesigned the actor network by adding a `scalingLayer` after the `tanhLayer` with `Scale = 0.75` and `Bias = 1.25`, which correctly maps the actor output from `[-1,1]` into `[0.5,2.0]`. After rebuilding the TD3 agent with this corrected actor, we repeated the `getAction()` tests and confirmed that the actor now produced different α and β values for different observation states, proving that the policy was finally responsive to the inputs. This debugging session was critical because it revealed that the apparent lack of learning was not caused by the reward function, disturbance profile, training duration, or TD3 algorithm itself, but by an action-scaling mismatch in the actor network that prevented the agent from ever generating meaningful adaptive gain corrections. This fix is likely the most significant technical breakthrough in the project so far because it restores the entire learning pathway from observations → actor → actions → PI gains → plant response → reward.
